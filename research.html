Previous Positions

Massachusetts Institute of Technology

While at MIT I did research in theoretical cosmology for several years in Prof. Alan Guth's Density Perturbations Group, under the direction of Prof. David Kaiser and alongside Ani Prabhu, Evangelos Sfakianakis, and Chanda Prescod-Weinstein. A popular model of the early universe invented by Prof. Guth is that of inflationary cosmology, in which the universe expanded exponentially quickly during the first instants after the Big Bang. However, this rapid expansion requires a large amount of energy, which must then be restored to matter fields to create particles so that the early universe is hot and dense. The first stage of this reheating of the universe is known as preheating.


We investigated a particular class of models of preheating in which multiple scalar fields coupled in a nonminimal way to the Ricci scalar. Such scalar fields are predicted by realistic models of high-energy physics in the early universe; the nonminimal couplings are the simplest renormalizable (mathematically consistent) interactions of scalar fields with Einstein gravity. In our papers (below), we show that the field driving inflation (the inflaton) also drives resonant particle production in the other scalar fields. We identify different regimes of values of certain parameters governing how strongly the fields couple, in which the dynamics of the resonances behave more or less strongly. Finally, we show that the effects of the Hubble expansion of the universe during this phase are also distinct depending on the values of the couplings.

Imperial College London

During the summer after my junior year at MIT I worked on a project in algebraic combinatorics applied to string theory during an eight-week summer exchange program with Prof. Amihay Hanany at Imperial College London. The abstract is as follows:


Superstring theory predicts a (9+1)-dimensional background spacetime and thus requires the orbifold compactification of six spatial dimensions, achieved by taking the quotient by a finite isometry group. Recent work in the context of quiver gauge theories has focused on enumeration of orbifolds of \(\mathbb{C}^n\) by an Abelian group, which in general can be expressed as a product of finite cyclic groups. The toric diagrams of these orbifolds are lattice simplices (generalized triangles) in \(\mathbb{R}^{n-1}\). We review crystallographic methods for enumerating inequivalent orbifolds via such toric diagrams and for obtaining analytic expressions for the resulting sequences by means of multiplicative sequences and generating functions. We then apply these methods to Abelian orbifolds of more general spaces with geometrically interesting toric diagrams, namely, the Platonic solids. Motivation for studying the Platonic solids comes from the study of quiver gauge theories; in the ADE classification of discrete subgroups of \(\text{SU}(2)\), the symmetry groups of the Platonic solids correspond to particularly simple quivers.
